The integrated scan reveals a uniquely challenging environment for Korea due to the convergence of multiple structural pressures:

**Export Dependency Amplification**: Korea's export-to-GDP ratio exceeding 40% means that the 2.6% global trade growth slowdown has outsized macroeconomic effects compared to more domestically oriented economies. The simultaneous occurrence of won depreciation (1,500 KRW/USD), semiconductor super-cycle demand, and geopolitical energy risk creates a volatile environment where positive and negative economic factors interact in complex and unpredictable ways.

**Chaebol Cascading Effects**: Korea's conglomerate-dominated economic structure means that strategic decisions by Samsung, SK, Hyundai, and LG groups have cascading effects across multiple sectors simultaneously. Samsung's semiconductor investment decisions affect not only the chip industry but also employment, tax revenue, real estate markets near fabrication facilities, and university research funding priorities. This interconnectedness amplifies both opportunities and risks.

**Demographic Existential Pressure**: Korea's world-lowest total fertility rate (estimated below 0.7 in 2026) creates a unique interpretive lens for all signals. AI automation, immigration policy, social welfare expansion, and healthcare robotics are not merely technology or policy signals -- they are existential responses to a demographic trajectory that threatens Korea's long-term economic viability. The government's 280+ policy response across 37 agencies reflects the perceived severity.

**Regulatory First-Mover Risk**: Korea's early AI ethics legislation positions the country as a regulatory pioneer in Asia, but the 3-month implementation gap between principles and guidelines creates first-mover disadvantage risk. The parallel struggles of the EU AI Act (8/27 states ready) suggest this is a global challenge, but Korea's smaller market size makes the competitive cost of regulatory uncertainty proportionally larger.

**Energy Security Vulnerability**: Korea's near-total dependence on imported energy (approximately 95% of primary energy) makes it uniquely exposed to the Middle East crisis. The offshore wind special act and broader renewable energy transition have strategic importance far beyond environmental policy -- they are fundamental to Korea's economic sovereignty and security.
