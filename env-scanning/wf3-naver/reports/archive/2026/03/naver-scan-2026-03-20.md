**Export Dependency**: Korea's export-to-GDP ratio exceeding 40% means that currency movements, trade policy shifts, and semiconductor market dynamics have outsized macroeconomic effects compared to more domestically oriented economies. The simultaneous occurrence of won depreciation, semiconductor super-cycle, and geopolitical energy risk creates a volatile environment where positive and negative factors interact unpredictably.

**Chaebol Structure**: Korea's conglomerate-dominated economy means that major decisions by Samsung, SK, Hyundai, and LG group companies have cascading effects across multiple sectors. Samsung's semiconductor investment decisions affect not only the chip industry but also Korean employment, tax revenue, real estate markets in fab-adjacent areas, and university research funding priorities.

**Demographic Urgency**: Korea's world-lowest total fertility rate (estimated below 0.7 in 2026) creates a unique lens through which all signals must be interpreted. AI automation, immigration policy, social welfare expansion, and healthcare robotics are not just technology or policy signals -- they are existential responses to a demographic trajectory that threatens Korea's long-term economic viability.

**Regulatory Innovation**: Korea's early enactment of AI ethics legislation positions the country as a regulatory pioneer, but also creates first-mover disadvantage risk if implementation guidelines do not provide sufficient clarity for industry compliance. The balance between innovation promotion and ethical safeguarding will define Korea's AI competitiveness for the next decade.

**Energy Security Vulnerability**: Korea's near-total dependence on imported energy (approximately 95% of primary energy) makes it uniquely vulnerable to the Middle East crisis and underscores the strategic importance of the offshore wind special act and broader renewable energy transition.
